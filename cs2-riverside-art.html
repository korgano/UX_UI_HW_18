The Problem

Riverside Art Center aims to host local art events and connect local artists with the community. We observed that users have a difficult time navigating the booking process on the event page and understanding the benefits of membership. This is causing users to not attend events, artists not being seen, and the loss of donations.

One problem we found was there was no way to actually make a reservation or book an event on the website. It would simply add the event to your Google calendar.

Aesthetically, there seemed to a number of issues, like the odd placement of the arrow above donations on desktop, that might be due to errors in responsive web design configuration.

Researching the town of Wapakoneta, Ohio revealed that it was the hometown of Neil Armstrong, explaining some of the visual motifs of the existing site. We e-mailed the Riverside Art Center, hoping to get an interview, but we received no response.

    4 interviews were conducted with people who engage with non-profit art organizations. We learned that:
  • Most people go to the sites to search for events.
  • Users' pain points were finding locations for the events, details about the event, and if the event was family friendly.
  • Users like booking and finding information through social media, and socials are rarely up to date.
  • Users like to know what they are going to be doing at the event and if it will be messy.

We also conducted a paid Google Survey targeting United States residents, obtaining 15 responses.

    Key Takeaways:
  • Social media and websites are vital to learning about art non-profits.
  • Donation buttons might be seen as “pushy” if they’re omnipresent.

We conducted 4 competitor analysis, focusing on other art non-profits. The Richmond Art Center website provided a lot of influences, namely the combined logo and primary navigation bar, and the three item nav cards. It also made good use of screen real estate, especially in comparison with the existing RAC site.

The Women in the Arts site’s header communicated the organization’s tagline and organization type immediately to the user. It also features a rotating set of images with information about the organization, current and upcoming events, and exhibitions. These features were brought into the final design.

In the River Arts District competitor analysis, we noticed the ability to filter artists by medium, and the upcoming events persisting across pages, which showed the priorities of the site. The footer from this page inspired our footer, and the navigation bar also, with the current tab being underlined.

We also did analysis of the Cultural Arts Center in Ohio. It featured a navigation bar that shrinks when scrolling, and a drop down menu. The primary elements we took from this site was the subscribe option in the footer.

Definition

Our user persona is of Henry Dali, a lifelong resident of Waponkeneta, Ohio. Based on our research of the organization, we chose to make an older persona to match their user demographics.

He says "I want to know when and where events are", he thinks "family focused events will feel safe," he does not feel obligated to donate if the organization doesn't do a good job of explaining what they do and what the money is used for, and he does like to donate to good artists.

Ideation

Now that we had an idea of what the user wants, we could start the process of determining what the business needs.

Naturally, this process was made more difficult by not having direct contact with our stakeholders.

We instead had to examine the site and their social media pages to determine their priorities and where they would intersect with the users'. Based on that analysis, we determined that events and classes would be the main priority for both our stakeholders and the users.

We used a value proposition to ensure the website was designed around customer values and customer needs. We wanted to make sure our users know what they were getting when they come to the site, by making it easier to navigate and making the site more readable.

The user flow, reflecting our storyboard, is Henry Dali booking a reservation for his family at the Riverside Art Center.

The flow was designed to be a streamlined way to book a reservation from the website. There was no way to make reservations on the existing site, so we created this from scratch.

Based on our research, the Riverside Art Center's current method of determining how many people are planning on attending an event or class consists of going to Facebook and clicking "I'm interested in this event."

By creating this reservation system, we not only allow users who don't use Facebook to sign up for events, we allow RAC to have a better idea of how many people will attend each event, and allow them to collect any payments required beforehand.

Prototyping

We iterated the site map several times, before settling on this information architecture. Given how little content there is, what would normally be secondary pages on a site map are instead sections of the primary page, with what look like tertiary pages being our secondary pages.

During the process, we realized that the original site’s About page content was nearly identical to content on the home page. That led us to ultimately merge the visual elements into the home page, and the text into the Join Us page.

We didn't deviate too far from the original site map, because navigation was one of its strong suites. The main issues were in layout and functionality.
